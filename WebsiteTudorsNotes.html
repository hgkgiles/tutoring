How did Henry VII become King, and what situation did Henry face from day one?

  1. Henry VII DID NOT become the King by being the eldest son of the last King. Instead he became King by killing the last king
  1. Henry was distantly related to a previous King of England, Edward III, but there were lots of people who could claim to be next in line to be the King, well ahead of Henry,  
  2. The last King was called Richard III. He was killed by Henry VII in a battle called the ‘Battle of Bosworth’ in 1485.
  3. Why does this matter?
  1. Because if you become the new king and you’re not the son/brother of the last king, many people may not think you should be allowed to be the king. AND THIS MEANS THE KING WILL HAVE LOTS OF REBELLIONS by people who don’t accept him as king.
  1. If your headmaster was killed by a random person on the street, and then this random person became the new Headmaster, would you allow him to tell you what to do?
  1. A major part of Henry VII's first few years as King was trying to stop people who don’t think he is the LEGITIMATE king trying to take over and choose a different king.        
  1. KEY WORD: ‘Legitimate’
  2. Legitimate kings have the RIGHT to tell you what to do (e.g. they are the eldest son of the last King). ILLEGITIMATE kings are seen as NOT HAVING THE RIGHT to be the king (e.g. they only way they are the king is that they killed the last king).
  1. Many of the family of the last King (Richard III) were still alive after Richard was killed, and these were the people that were most likely to violently take Henry VII’s power away from him.
  1. The family of Richard III, who was the King Henry VII killed, was called ‘York’
  2. The family of Henry VII was called ‘Lancaster’ (he was also part of the Tudor family)
  3. It was the ‘Yorkist claimants’ that were most likely to overthrow Henry VII as they were family members of the last king, Richard III.
  1. Key WORD: ‘claimant’
  1. A claimant is someone who thinks they have the right to be king - if there are no sons of the last king, then the nephew, or cousins, or second cousins should become the next king. Every ‘claimant’ can be ranked in how much right they have to be the next King.
  1. REMEMBER: Henry VII did not have a good reason to be the King, as he was only really the King because he killed the last King, Richard. He wasn’t the son or nephew or cousin of the last King, Richard III
  1. The best ‘claimant’ - the person with the most right to be the king instead of Henry VII were Richard III’s SISTERS and NEPHEWS (RIchard didn’t have any sons). They were all members of the YORK family
  1. SISTERS:

Elizabeth of York

  1. Richard III’s eldest sister
  1. NEPHEWS:
  1. Edward, Earl of Warwick
  1. He was only 10 years old when Richard III was killed. BUT… he was still a major threat, as people who wanted to remove Henry VII could attack Henry in a battle, and claim that they were only trying to put the LEGITIMATE and lawful king on the throne.This would help attract supporters.
  1. John de la Pole
  1. He promised to be loyal to Henry VII, so not a major threat
  2. However… he would later break his promise and join a rebellion against Henry
  1. Henry got rid of the threat of these family members of Richard III in clever ways.
  1. Elizabeth of York was dealt with by MARRYING HER.
  1. This meant that the York family felt like they had a share of power, and that the children of Henry VII and Elizabeth of York would be part of the York family.
  1. Edward, Earl of Warwick was dealt with by locking him in the Tower of London (a prison).
  1. He also helped make himself look MORE LEGITIMATE (rightfully the king) by ensuring that parliament immediately passed a law saying that he and his future sons were the rightful kings of England.
  2. However, the danger to Henry VII was NOT COMPLETELY REMOVED.
  1. There were still enough people in Henry’s Kingdom who wanted to kill him, and they would stop at nothing until he was dead.

Who were the biggest threats to Henry VII?

  1. THREAT ONE
  1. Right after Henry VII became king, three old supporters of Richard III tried to organise groups of men to attack Henry as he went on a journey to York in the north of England
  1. It was organised by three men: Francis Lovell, Henry Stafford, and Thomas Stafford.
  1. This FAILED
  1. There were not enough supporters of the rebellion
  2. Those that did support it were promised a pardon by Henry VII (an official forgiveness and protection from execution if they gave up the rebellion), and so when it seemed like the rebellion might fail due to lack of support, this gave the rebels an easy way to back out with zero consequences.
  3. Henry Stafford was executed
  4. The rebellion was NOT A SERIOUS THREAT
  1. But it did show that there was opposition to Henry VII right from the beginning of his reign - the rebels were not unhappy with Henry’s new laws and ideas, but with the fact that Henry was King at all. 
  2. This is a reminder that there was not much Henry could do to keep everyone happy, even if he was a good king.
  1. THREAT TWO
  1. An invasion of England by rebels that lived in Ireland and wanted Henry VII replaced.
  1. Ireland was ruled by England at this time, and a powerful lord in Ireland called the Earl of Kildare wanted the English to give up control of Ireland.
  1. They knew that replacing Henry VII with a king who would let Ireland be free would make Ireland free.
  2. If they could get all the old supporters of Richard III in England to suddenly rise up and join the invasion that would be enough to kill Henry VII
  1. The idea was to replace Henry VII with the nephew of Richard III (Edward Earl of Warwick)
  1. BUT… Edward Earl of Warwick was LOCKED UP IN THE TOWER
  1. SO the rebels took a random child from Ireland called Lambert Simnel and made him pretend to be Edward!
  1. Having the (fake) Edward Earl of Warwick at the head of the rebellion would help the rebels gain supporters who liked the dead king Richard III after they landed in England
  1. The rebels landed in Lancashire in the north of England in JUNE 1487 and started marching towards London to kill Henry
  2. BUT! They were DEFEATED at the Battle of Stoke.
  1. This was a very serious threat to Henry VII’s rule, even though it ended up failing.
  1. Why was it a serious threat?
  1. The rebels were given a huge amount of money and 2000 soldiers by one of the richest people in Europe, Margaret of Burgundy
  1. Margaret was a relative of Richard III and wanted revenge for Richard III being killed by Henry VII
  1. The whole of Ireland supported the invasion
  2. Many people in England really believed that the Edward Earl of Warwick, the child leader of the rebellion (not really in charge but acting as a magnet to gain more supporters), was the REAL Edward (the real one was actually locked in the Tower of London), and deserved to be the king instead of Henry
  1. Why did it fail?
  1. Many people in England didn’t join the rebellion, as there had been too many wars in the 1400s already, and people were tired of the king always getting killed, which lead to chaos
  2. The Irish invaders were scary, and the English feared them, so wouldn’t join them, even though they didn’t really believe Henry VII was the rightful king.
  1. THREAT THREE
  1. There was an attempted invasion of England by Yorkist supporters that wanted to remove Henry VII and make a young man called Perkin Warbeck ‘King Richard IV’ (4th).
  1. Perkin Warbeck was just a young boy who worked for a merchant. But, some Irish Rebels thought they could use him to pretend to be the son of  the old king Edward IV to attract supporters/rebels against Henry VII, and then all team up and invade England to replace Henry VII.
  1. The idea was to destabilise England by killing the King, so Irealnd could be free. But, the York family supporters would also benefit by getting their family back to being the family in charge of England
  1. NOTE: Perkin Warbeck was only pretending to be the son of Edward IV, who was actually dead… (he was convinced to pretend as it would convince York supporters to join in)
  1. Perkin Warbeck got a ship to the ‘Low Countries’ (nowadays part of the Netherlands) to meet up with Margaret of Burgundy, who was one of the richest women in Europe, and a member of the York family (remember the Lambert Simnel rebellion?)
  1. This would help the Irish men who were working with him get some money to hire soldiers
  1. Warbeck and the men working with him made a deal with the man in charge of the mediaeval country called ‘Holy Roman Empire’ (which is now called Germany). He was called Maximillian.
  1. The deal said that if Maximilian I (1st) helped Warbeck become the king of England, then, when Warbeck died, Maximilliam would be allowed to become king.
  1. Many supporters of the York family in England got ready to help Warbeck when he arrived
  1. One of these supporters was working for Henry VII, but secretly a supporter of the York Family - Sir William Stanley 
  2. Warbeck and his friends invaded England at a place called Deal in Kent, but their soldiers were immediately defeated because Henry VII had spies hidden among the invaders who had already told Henry VII the invasion was coming.
  3. Perkin Warbeck fled to Ireland, and then Scotland.
  1. How serious was this threat?
  1. This was not extremely serious, but still a real danger to Henry VII
  1. It was serious because:
  1. It had support, and therefore lots of money, from Margaret of Burgundy
  2. A man who worked very closely with the King (Sir William Stanley) was secretly involved, showing that even Henry’s closest friends could turn against him.
  1. However it wasn’t extremely serious:
  1. It was easily stopped because Henry had spies (eg Sir Robert Clifford who betrayed the invaders)
  2. When Warbeck escaped to Ireland and then to Scotland, almost no one was willing to join him to continue his quest to overthrow Henry VII
  1. He did begin travelling to Cornwall and joined up with some men there who wanted to rebel over high taxes, but he ended up being too afraid to go through with it and abandoned the plan.
  2. He eventually was captured by Henry and executed.
  1. THREAT FOUR
  1. There was a rebellion in Cornwall
  2. Henry’s war with Scotland caused a major rebellion over taxes in Cornwall.
  3. This was a serious problem as the Cornish rebels marched all the way to London, and there was almost no army in England to stop them (the army was in Scotland)
  1. The army got back from Scotland just in time
  1. Perkin Warbeck had joined the rebels, but was captured and executed
  1. This was the end of the threat from Warbeck.

How well did Henry VII deal with these threats?

  1. There were THREE ways in which Henry dealt with the threats WELL
  1. FIRST GOOD THING        
  1. Henry married a member of the family that was a threat: the Yorkist family. He married Elizabeth of York
  1. This was clever as it ensured that Henry VII’s children would also now have a member of the York family as a parent (Elizabeth), and thus have Yorkist ‘blood.’
  2. This meant the Yorkists didn't feel left out of power, and would be less likely to rebel
  1. SECOND GOOD THING
  1. Henry was not to mean to those that rebelled against him
  1. Many of the people who rebelled were not punished very severely
  1. Eg. Lambert Simnel was not executed even though he led a rebellion.
  1. Instead he was given a job in Henry’s palace looking after the falcons.
  1. Most of the members of the Cornish Rebellion were allowed to go free unpunished - only the leaders were executed.
  2. This made Henry look calm and confident, and not scared by the threats, which made his enemies more afraid of him.
  1. He wasn’t stupid enough to NEVER execute the rebels - he did it only when he NEEDED to
  1. E.G the Earl of Warwick was beheaded and Perkin Warbeck was Hanged
  2. Eg. Sir William Stanley, who had helped Perkin Warbeck, was executed. Stanley had been actually working as part of Henry VII’s household, so the threat was very dangerous, and Henry was right to be tough.
  1. THIRD GOOD THING
  1. Henry ensured he had spies giving him information about the rebels
  1. This was how he knew about Perkin Warbeck’s invasion in advance, and was able to have soldiers at the landing point ready and waiting.

Other ways Henry secured his power

  1. Henry was King in 1485, and he managed to defend himself against the threats described above. But he did other things to secure his place as King:

Five Key ways Henry Secured his throne.

  1. FIRST WAY
  1. He made sure that the official beginning of his rule as King began the day BEFORE the battle of Bosworth
  1. This meant he could arrest and put on trial anyone who had fought against him, as they had officially committed TREASON.
  2. This was clever, as those who had fought Henry at Bosworth weren’t ACTUALLY committing treason as Henry wasn’t the King AT THE TIME OF BOSWORTH.
  1. This was good because trying to fight/kill a king is treason, and has much bigger punishments, and is quicker and easier when the traitor is put on trial.
  1. SECOND WAY
  1. Henry made sure that his coronation (special ceremony where a man is officially made king) happened before parliament met for the first time under Henry VII’s time as king.
  1. IF Henry had waited until parliament met AND THEN had his coronation ceremony, it might have looked like parliament was needed to ‘give the OK’ to Henry being King
  2. This ensured that Henry looked more powerful, and did not look like he relied on anyone for his position as King
  1. Important info:
  1. In the Tudor times, parliament didn’t sit and meet everyday, all year round, like they do today. Instead, they met only for a month or so at a time, once or twice a year, or not even that - they only met when the king called them.
  1. THIRD WAY
  1. Henry VII took the land and money from many of his Yorkist enemies (the people he defeated at the Battle of Bosworth).
  2. This made him RICH
  3. Rich kings can govern more easily, as they don’t have to ask parliament to give them money. Parliament would ask laws to be changed in exchange for this gift of money, so a king who never asks for money can keep the laws just how they want, and ignore parliament.
  1. PLUS
  1. Henry got parliament to guarantee that he would receive all the tax money from imports and exports, and all the tax on wine and wool.
  2. These taxes were called ‘Tonnage and Poundage’
  1. FOURTH WAY
  1. He successfully had two sons, Arthur and Henry (later Henry VIII (the eighth))’
  2. Having one son would mean that if Henry 7th died, the Tudor family would be able to keep power, as Henry, his son, would become King automatically
  3. Having TWO sons meant that even if one of his sons died when he was young (e.g. of a disease), they would be a spare son who could still take power after Henry VII died.
  1. PLUS
  1. He was clever to name his eldest son ‘Arthur,’ as it reminded people of King Arthur, a mythical king from long ago, and made Henry VII look like he was connected to the past
  2. In reality, he had almost no connection with the past, as he had only become king by killing the last king.

How did Henry change the way England was governed?

There are THREE ways that Henry changed how England was governed:

ONE - Henry took more control over how he raised money

TWO - Henry changed how his Council (advisors) worked

THREE - Henry changed how he dealt with law and order

  1. WAY HE CHANGED GOVERNMENT ONE
  1. Henry made the government RICHER by RAISING MORE MONEY
  1. There were FOUR sources of money that Henry made MORE PROFITABLE for him and the government:
  1. Rent from land owned by the King
  2. Taxation from imports and exports 
  3. Profits from FINING people who broke the law, and FEES from people who wanted justice in the court system.
  4. Special money that was owed to the King due to the ‘feudal system’ (an ancient system going back to William the Conqueror)

WAY ONE (MONEY) PART ONE - RENT FROM LAND

  1. Whoever becomes the King of England inherits lots of land from day one, but there were lots of ways the money coming in from land was improved:
  1. Henry INCREASED the land he owned, making the government MORE POWERFUL
  1. Henry simply stole more land from his enemies        
  1. Henry was ruthless in taking land and he knew how important long term income was to his power.
  2. E.g when William Stanley rebelled and was executed (he supported Perkin Warbeck) Henry VII got all his lands - this added £1000 a year to Henry’s regular income.
  1. He even took land from people he claimed were friends of Richard the III (the last king), but who weren’t actually Richard’s friends
  2. Total income of land quadrupled over Henry’s reign from £10,000 a year to £40,000 a year (in today’s money this is hundreds of millions of pounds)
  3. Henry stopped the tendency of previous Kings to sell land to get short term money (but long term loss of income)
  1. This old policy was called ‘alienation’ (which means to give away)
  1. Unlike the last Kings, he MADE SURE people actually paid rent
  1. The more land Henry had the more he could give as gifts to ensure people were loyal/worked hard for him, and the more ‘ordinary revenue’ he had available and thus the more people he could pay to do what he wanted (eg soldiers/government workers) and the more expensive clothes and palaces he had to look impressive.
  1. Key Term:
  1. ‘Ordinary revenue’ and ‘extraordinary revenue’ are the two types of income (revenue means income). Ordinary revenue is the money that comes in every year, guaranteed. Extraordinary revenue is the money that is a one off payment (grant or loan) from parliament. Parliament would agree to give the king money in exchange for some control over what laws are made.

        

WAY ONE (MONEY) PART TWO - INCREASING MONEY FROM TAX ON IMPORTS

  1. Taxes on people from other countries selling goods in England are called ‘customs’
  2. Henry was much stricter on people who were avoiding tax, and was much more organised in how much was charged. This fits with his government being much more organised with money collecting (‘revenue’) overall. Henry did many things to increase the money from customs:
  1. He changed the law to make it harder to avoid the tax (smuggling is when people secretly trade with foreigners without paying tax)
  1. 1487: the 'Act Prohibiting Coastal Trading Without Customers Certificates’
  2. He twice updated the tax rates to be higher in (1503 and 1507)
  1. He made sure trade with foreign countries increased - the more English people traded with foreign countries, the more they paid in tax to Henry
  1. Two trade deals with Spain (who ruled the area now called the Netherlands, who England traded wool with a lot)
  1. Medina del Campo (1490)
  2. Magnus intercursus (1496)
  1. Henry increased the income from customs (tax on imports) from £33,000 at the beginning of his rule, to £38,000 by the end

WAY ONE (MONEY) PART THREE - PROFITS FROM FINING PEOPLE

  1. Henry was very ruthless in how much people were charged for various activities
  1. Anyone who had a male advisor (a ‘retainer’: almost like CEO of a company that you own but don’t run day to day) now needed a licence, and if you didn't buy one, you were fined £5. This raised a huge amount of money - five pounds was about £1000 in today's money.        
  1. ‘Retainers were gentlemen with ‘sir’ in their name’ who helped nobles (earl/duke etc) run their lives.
  1. Anyone who got caught smuggling now got a huge fine
  2. Anyone who was forgiven in one of the rebellions discussed above was fined, even people who had already been given the death penalty.
  3. Rich men (nobles/gentlemen) hunting/catching animals in a private forest were fined.
  1. Very often, the fines were used as a way of weakening his opponents by finding them and making them poor.
  1. For example he made the Earl of Oxford pay a £10,000 fine for having too many servants without a licence (‘retainers’).

WAY ONE (MONEY) PART FOUR - SPECIAL MONEY OWED DUE TO THE FEUDAL SYSTEM

  1. The feudal system had existed in England since 1066. It was a system where the King owns all the land in the country, but lends it to various members of his extended family (barons), and then asks them to give him soldiers/military service in return.
  2. The king would also ask rich people to pay certain fees when special things happened, like getting married, or changing who you will give money to when you die.
  1. These fees are called feudal rights
  2. Henry changed government by increasing the number of feudal rights he made people pay.
  1. This ‘feudal rights’ already existed before Henry, but many people, including the Kings before him, had forgotten they existed
  1. Henry set up an investigation (‘commission’) to see what he was entitled to
  2. He charged people if they had a son and wanted to leave money or land to them, but died before their son was 16.
  3. He ensures the nobles of England paid him money when his son Arthur got married, and when his daughter Margaret got married (this was his ‘right’ as a ‘feudal lord’)
  4. Giving land to the church (e.g. when you died and wanted to impress God) meant paying a big fee.
  1. Henry VII built up a reputation for being cruel and always wanting more money. They called him the ‘Winter King,’ but he greatly increased how much money came into the King.

WAY TWO - THE COUNCIL AND THE COURT

  1. Henry increased the size of his ‘council’. The King’s Council was a group of advisors who put Henry’s orders about how to run the country into practice. The King’s council was increased to 150 advisors, although only 40 tended to meet at one time.
  1. More advisors meant the King had lots of people to ensure all his new fines and taxes were being collected
  2. The most important councillor was Sir Reginal Bray
  3. Made sure Bray collected more money from the ‘Duchy of Lancaster’ (lots of properties owned by the King in an area called Lancaster) than previous Kings.
  1. Henry made a small part of the Council called ‘The Council Learned in the Law’ much more important.        
  1. The Council Learned was a way for Henry to ensure that he got all the money he was due, but importantly, it was a way of tricking and forcing people into paying money they didn’t really owe.
  2. The Council Learned was run by Richard Empson and Edmund Dudley, and was one of the most famous and hated changes Henry VII made to government.        
  3. Henry relied on lawyers (Empson and Dudley) to achieve his goals more than previous Kings. Lawyers are good at getting things done that others cannot.
  1. The Council Learned would accuse people of crimes they did not commit, and then fine them hundreds of pounds. They forced them to stay in prison until they paid
  2. E.g. Thomas Sunif was accused of murdering a newborn baby and fined 500 pounds. Their money helped Henry reign even more effectively, and this cruel method of making money made people afraid of Henry.
  1. Henry made sure to count all the money he collected inside the royal ‘household’ - that is not far away in an office, but near to his bedroom/personal space
  1. This made the monarchy even more ‘personal’ than before.
  1. The person who counted the money was now called the ‘Treasurer of the Chamber.’
  1. More than other Kings, such as his son Henry VIII, Henry VII ensured that he was not off hunting when the important decisions were made or money counted. He was right there at the centre of the action, totally in control.
  1. Henry personally read every single page of the accounts book, signing it with his initials (This is not what other Kings did).

WAY THREE - LAW AND ORDER

  1. Henry tried to take much more control over how easy it was for rich nobles to pay for ‘retainers’ (soldiers in private armies)
  1. Men could be paid money to join a private army commanded by the noble who paid.
  2. This had been so dangerous during the 1400s, before Henry VII, that many wars had started between the armies that these nobles had paid to create.
  1. It's really unsafe for a King to allow other rich people in the country to create private armies, as they can be used to attack the king. This is exactly what happened over and over during the ‘Wars of the Roses’
  1. SO Henry passed laws to stop any one paying a retainer/potentially creating a private army, unless they had a licence.
  1. This allowed Henry to monitor the nobles much better.
  1. Henry VII increased the role of Justices of the Peace
  1. The Justices of the Peace were like the police of the Tudor times
  1. They had existed before Henry VII, but Henry used them more as they were unpaid semi rich people who were a cheap and reliable way of ensuring people followed laws.
  1. E.g stealing a swan’s eggs or not paying a fine would be punished by a JP
  1. They had to follow orders as they got lots of respect for doing the job in their village, and would lose it if they lost their job as JP.
  1. Henry VII used more ‘bonds and recognizances’ more to keep people loyal
  1. Bonds and recognizances were basically large amounts of money that Henry would claim you owed, and then say ‘you don’t have to pay if you follow all the laws’.
  2. Empson and Dudley in the Council Learned in the Law made sure that these bonds and recognizances were applied to lots of people ruthlessly.

HOW SUCCESSFUL WERE HENRY’S REFORMS (HOW GOOD WAS HE AS A KING)

  1. GOOD THING ONE
  1. Henry made sure that he collected all the money that he could.
  1.  A rich king is a powerful King, and the lands, fines, feudal rights, bonds and recognizances, and tax on imports, were all greatly improved
  2. Previous Kings in the 1400s had lost some of their power because they kept asking parliament or the rich nobles for money, and had to change laws or policies in exchange. Henry DIDN’T do this.
  3. He also didn’t have too many wars, and parties, so he didn’t waste the money he collected.
  1. When he died he left his son Henry VIII two million pounds, which showed how successful all the money raising (and not spending too much) had been.
  1. GOOD THING TWO
  1. Henry ensured that people were afraid of him
  1. Empson and Dudley from the Council Learned in the Law were hated by the people, but also feared. People knew that if they broke laws, rebelled, or didn’t pay money they owed there would definitely be consequences
  2. People were also impressed by Henry’s fancy clothes, palaces, and feasts/jousting (a sport played by rich people which is expensive and impressive) to earn enough respect and awe from the people
  1. He didn’t allow any noble (rich person from birth with lots of land) to look anywhere near as important as him
  2. However, he didn’t spend too much money doing this, just enough to make people be impressed, and no more.
  3. Many Ambassadors (visitors from foreign countries sent by foreign kings) from Venice and Milan reported back that his children’s weddings and his own, as well as his coronation were very impressive
  1. This was extra important as he was only the king because he killed the last king, so really needed to win respect.
  1. GOOD THING THREE
  1. Henry didn’t spend his time hunting or chasing women. He worked hard and put himself at the centre of the business of government
  2. For example, he personally checked the account book every day, and did not choose a single individual to run the country day to day, like his son Henry VIII did (Cardinal Wolsey and then Thomas Cromwell)
  3. AFTER A PERIOD OF CHAOS (1400s) Henry restored the government of England to order and stability through careful hard work, which is good.

  1. BAD THING ONE - HENRY’S FINANCIAL REFORMS COULD HAVE BEEN EVEN BETTER
  1. Henry didn’t increase the income from customs as much as he could have.
  1. This is partly because Henry banned trading with the ‘Low Countries’ in 1493 (the modern day Netherlands, which was England's biggest trading partner) in order to force the Emperor Maximillian to stop protecting Perkin Warbeck  
  1. Perkin Warbeck was a threat to Henry (see the section on securing Henry VII’s place as king)
  1. So, for a while, trade massively declined and therefore tax income declined, as there was less trade to tax.
  2. Henry might have chosen to not punish Maximillian, the Emperor hiding Warbeck, and favour the merchants who wanted to keep trading, but he didn’t.
  1. Henry also didn’t stop all the smuggling that was happening. So lots of tax money was still not paid.

BAD THING TWO - HE WAS HATED BY MANY

  1. Henry had a reputation for being a cruel King, who ruthlessly forced people to pay him money they didn’t owe. Henry became known as the ‘Winter King, ’ as he was cold, and harsh.
  2. This is especially due to Empson and Dudley, the people who ran the Council Learned (see above)

BAD THING THREE - HE DIDN'T CHANGE MUCH ABOUT THE COUNCIL AND PARLIAMENT

  1. Henry VII didn’t modernise the King's council, or allow parliament to have a big/permanent role in government. Therefore, although Henry’s reforms made him more money, and helped keep the country stable, the government remained mediaeval.
  1. Thomas Cromwell under the next King Henry VIII made much bigger reforms that made the government of England much better and more efficient.
  1. Some have said that Henry invited lots of ‘middle class’ people into his government to help him, and that was a sign he was good at ruling. HOWEVER, this is not actually true. Most of Henry’s Council were noblemen or Gentlemen

BAD THING FOUR - HE DID NOT SOLVE THE PROBLEM OF THE CHURCH HAVING TOO MUCH POWER

  1. Finally, the big problem of the Church being seen as corrupt and greedy, and being a sort of second source of power in England, was not solved.        
  1. There was lots of corruption in the church, and the priests and monks got away with not following all the laws. For example, if a priest killed someone, then they could get away with only being punished by the church, not the King, as long as it was only their first offence.
  1. Henry VIII did solve this in the 1530s, so might be seen as better by comparison.

OVERALL (good as a conclusion to an essay)

  • Henry VII was an excellent King. He saw that the most important thing was to bring in as much money as possible, as this would save him from having to ask rich people in the country to loan or grant him money, weakening his power relative to theirs. He made this money work well for him, as he always appeared Kingly and far more important and powerful than anyone else in the Kingdom, which many previous kings of the previous century failed to do, which led to civil war. Section Four: English Society during Henry VII

In this section you need to know the answer to just one question: How much did English society change 1485-1509?

WAY IT CHANGED ONE - Henry took more strict control over the nobles (dukes/earls)

  1. FIRSTLY, ‘Bastard feudalism’ was made less dangerous:
  2. From 1066 (William the conqueror) to 1166 a system called ‘feudalism’. But this was gradually replaced with a more dangerous system called bastard feudalism, between 1166 and Henry VII’s time.
  1. Normal feudalism: this was a system where the King owned all the land, but gave 90 percent of it away to his extended family/friends (hundreds of ‘barons’) in exchange for military service.
  1. They get land, but have to provide a mini army for the King when needed. And how did the barons get soldiers? The barons gave THEM land. It was like a mini pyramid over the whole country of ‘you give me land I give you loyalty and service in battle.’
  1. Bastard Feudalism: this was when it stopped being ‘I give you land and you fight for me in a battle’ and became ‘I give you money and you fight for me in a battle.
  1. This made it easier to raise BIG armies. BUT…
  2. IT WAS EXTREMELY DANGEROUS
  1. If you can only exchange LAND for military service, it's harder for big private armies to form that can threaten the King. you can predict who has the land and therefore the soldiers, and ensure they are loyal.
  2. BUT if MONEY is what people start exchanging… who knows who might get enough money to raise an army, and then use it to take over England.
  1. SECONDLY, Henry tried to take much more control over how easy it was for rich nobles to pay for ‘retainers’
  1. RETAINERS were the people who were paid money to join a private army, or just be an advisor or helper to a noble, giving the noble more power.
  2. This had been so dangerous during the 1400s, before Henry VII, that many wars had started between the armies that these nobles had paid to create.
  1. It's really unsafe for a King to allow other rich people in the country to create private armies, as they can be used to attack the king. This is exactly what happened over and over during the ‘Wars of the Roses’
  1. SO Henry passed laws to stop any one paying a retainer/potentially creating a private army, unless they had a licence.
  1. In 1486 nobles had to swear an oath directly to Henry to promise to not secretly obtain soldiers
  2. The rule that you needed a licence was brought in in 1504
  3. This allowed Henry to monitor the nobles much better.
  1. For example, Lord Bergvenny was fined £100,000 for having retainers without the special licence.
  1. FINALLY Henry VII used more ‘bonds and recognizances’ more to keep people loyal
  1. Bonds and recognizances were basically large amounts of money that Henry would claim you owed, and then say ‘you don’t have to pay if you follow all the laws’.
  2. Empson and Dudley in the Council Learned in the Law made sure that these bonds and recognizances were applied to lots of people ruthlessly.

WAY IT CHANGED TWO - Gentry become more important

  • The Gentry were men who were rich, but did NOT have one of the five special titles that make someone a ‘noble’ (Duke, Marquess, Earl, Viscount, and Baron)
  • Instead they had the title ‘sir’
  • In Henry VII’s England the gentry became slightly more important
  • Firstly, some gentry were now given the job of ‘justice of the peace’
  • The Justices of the Peace were like the police of the Tudor times
  • They had existed before Henry VII, but Henry used them more as they were unpaid semi rich people who were a cheap and reliable way of ensuring people followed laws.
  • E.g stealing a swan’s eggs or not paying a fine would be punished by a JP
  • They had to follow orders as they got lots of respect for doing the job in their village, and would lose it if they lost their job as JP.
  • Secondly, gentry became more likely to be in the King’s council, alongside nobles.
  • For example, Sir Reginald Bray was Henry’s main advisor. He was a member of the gentry (notice the title ‘sir’?)

WAY IT CHANGED THREE - the Church leaders were less likely to be members of the elite

  • To be a leading figure in the church meant being at the top of a pyramid:
  • Priest (normal, has one church), Bishop (very important, has whole cathedral), Archbishop (has one of two top cathedrals, York and Canterbury), Cardinal (most important church leader in England), Pope (world leader of church, there's only one Pope)
  • One of the highest ranked church leaders under Henry VII was, for the first time, a member of the ‘yeomanry’ (rich farmers, not even with ‘sir’ as a title),
  • His name was Richard Fox.
  • He was the Bishop of Exeter (very high rank, in charge of whole cathedral)
  • Another high ranking leader was from the ‘lesser gentry,’ which means that he was considered a member of the gentry, but was not a knight, so didn't have the title ‘sir’
  • His name was John Morton. He rose up the ranks to become  cardinal, the highest rank except for the Pope.
  • Top nobles who became bishops/archbishops would have been harder to control, so this gave Henry more control over religion.

WAY IT CHANGED FOUR - the two rebellions under Henry VII

  1. BOTH rebellions, the Yorkshire Rebellion and the Cornish Rebellion, were caused by anger about special taxation (‘extraordinary revenue’) raised for wars (Yorkshire: tax for Brittany war, Cornish: tax for Scottish war)
  2. The first rebellion was less serious
  1. It was called the ‘Yorkshire Rebellion’ of 1489
  1. The rebels managed to kill a very important noble, the earl of Northumberland, who was seen as someone who supported high taxes.
  1. The second rebellion, the Cornish rebellion, was more serious:
  1. They had lots of men - 15,000
  2. They marched all the way to London when the King’s army was fighting in Scotland.
  1. Henry had to send back Laud Daubney to stop the rebels from taking over the country.
  1. The teamed up with Perkin Warbeck (rebel and ‘pretend’ king Richard IV)

SO… did England change much under Henry VII? Not really…

  1. FIRST REASON IT DIDN’T REALLY CHANGE MUCH - Bastard feudalism and retainers weren’t really stopped, just made slightly less dangerous.
  1. Henry couldn’t be too tough on the Nobles’ desire to have ‘retainers’ as he didn’t want to make them so angry they started a war.
  1. Yes Bergevennay was fined £100,000 (10 million pounds in today's money), but Henry only collected £1,000 in the end… so not THAT touch on the nobles.
  1. ALSO - the bonds and recognizances weren’t that new, as they had been used by previous kings to try and control the nobles…

  1. SECOND REASON IT DIDN’T REALLY CHANGE MUCH - ultimately the gentry didn’t become that much more powerful under Henry VII.        
  1.  There were still lots of nobles (Dukes and Earls, etc.) in the King’s Council
  2. The Gentry had had power and influence before Henry VII
  1. For example the Justices of the Peace had existed before Henry VII - Henry just created more of them than previous Kings
  1. THIRD REASON IT DIDN’T REALLY CHANGE MUCH - there were only two rebellions, and both were quite minor
  1. The Yorkshire rebellion only killed one earl, and was very easily defeated by Henry VII
  2. The Cornish Rebellion was more dangerous, but easily defeated by Henry (who sent Lord Daubney to crush it)
  1. FOURTH REASON IT DIDN’T REALLY CHANGE MUCH - the main feature of Henry VII’s reign was that everyone had enough food to eat, so things remained stable
  1. It tends to be that big social change comes when there are famines, or times of great population growth, and therefore hunger.
  2. BUT
  1. Under Henry VII the population didn’t change much, and there were no famines (known as ‘subsistence crises’)

Conclusion:

  • Overall, Henry VII’s England saw some important changes to the way the nobility were controlled, and some more key figures rising up from the peasantry and the lesser gentry, but it didn’t change that much. What is most memorable about 1485-1509, is that it was a time of great stability when compared to the violent changes that happened under Henry VIII’s reign. The fact that the population remained stable, and everyone had enough food certainly made big social changes less likely.

Section Four: Economic Development under Henry VIII

WAY IT CHANGED ONE - Prices more than doubled 1509-1547

  • 'Inflation' is where prices go up.
  • This is caused by there being MORE money going round but THE SAME amount of stuff to buy.
  • There was more money because Wolsey and then Henry VIII 'debased the coinage'
  • This means that thousands of silver coins held by the King were melted down, and then mixed with a less expensive metal, and remade into coins
  • This happened under Cardinal Wolsey (his own decision not Henry's) in 1526, and under Henry himself in 1540.
  • This was to pay for wars. Wolsey had a major war to pay for against France in 1526, and increased the value of his coins by £500,000, and then Henry had ANOTHER war with France in 1543, which was also paid for by melting coins.
  • The problem is - the King gets a short term advantage, as he made six times as many coins by melting them down, but they had 1/6th as much silver in them.
  • In the Tudor times, no one understood what made prices go up. People thought everything had a 'just price' set by God, and if prices went up then someone (merchants/the rich) were changing prices deliberately to enrich themselves.
  • However, in reality it was Henry/Wolsey debasing the coins
  • The new coins were clearly lighter and worth less, so people clung on to the old coins if they had them, and merchants would only accept the new coins if people paid twice as many,
  • Between 1500 and 1540 prices rose by 50 percent. Between 1540 and 1560 they doubled. By 1600 prices were five and a half times as high as 1500.
  • Overall, this means that wages also go down, as even if people are paid the same, they can buy less and less with the money as prices go up and up.
  • Bad harvests in 1520 and 1527 made this even worse.

WAY IT CHANGED TWO- The way people farmed changed (due to the inflation)

  • 90 percent of the population of England were farmers, so these changes made a big difference.
  • Higher prices meant that the best way to make money from farming changed:
  • Before, renting the land you owned to another peasant for a fixed amount every month made sense. But, as prices rose at the markets, the only way to keep up was to raise rents you were charging as well.
  • BUT raising rents couldn't go on forever, as eventually people couldn't afford to pay them
  • SO people stopped renting out land and farmed it directly themselves (by paying workers)
  • People before had owned lots of little strips of land (as it had been in the mediaeval times). They now made all their land come together into one, and then farmed sheep or cattle on them
  • You can't farm sheep on little strips of land all separated/scattered around.
  • So people who owned land, bought the land next to their strips, and sold the land scattered far away, so that there was room for sheep.
  • This was because selling sheep was 'inflation proof' as you could simply increase the price you sell the sheep for as prices rose. This was not as easy if you rented out little strips to other people, and farmed a few yourself.
  • ALSO
  • The new more efficient and advanced farming methods (see right below this) REQUIRED the land to be grouped together into much larger plots.
  • More efficient techniques for farming were invented and learned by farmers
  • For example, Thomas Tusser's 'Five Hundred Points of Husbandry' was published in 1557 (10 years after Henry died but the techniques were picked up by farmers over his whole reign)
  • Husbandry = animal farming

WAY IT CHANGED THREE - The cloth trade with Europe massively increased

  • Export of cloth increased three times 1500-1550
  • There were two reason this trade increased
  • REASON ONE
  • The 'great inflation' in England (caused by the coin melting) was actually GOOD for trade with other countries
  • In 1500 £1 was worth 32 Flemish coins
  • In 1522 £1 was worth 26 Flemish coins
  • in 1551 £1 was worth 13 Flemish coins
  • In order to pay English merchants, the Antwerp merchants would need to convert their own money into pounds.
  • The Flemish merchants would need to exchange less and less of their own money for one English pound.
  • This mean that if an English Merchant in Antwerp asked for £1 of payment for some cloth, the Flemish merchants would feel like they were getting that pound of cloth cheaper and cheaper between 1500 and 1550
  • Therefore if the pound decreases in value (due to the melting/debasing of the coins), then the Flemish merchants can buy twice as much cloth for the same money, which means that trade increases.
  • REASON TWO
  • In Spain, prices rose over 1500-1550.
  • This was because Spain had more and more gold being brought back from their discovery of South America.
  • This meant gold was less rare, and you needed more gold to pay for the same amount of goods.
  • SO
  • English merchants could sell their cloth for higher prices in Spain, than in England, which meant they would 'buy cheap' in England and 'sell expensive' in Spain.
  • This increased how profitable trade was, so trade increased.
  • Other new markets began to open up during this time:
  • Africa, America (the 'New World') became a place that trade could occur
  • The Empire Venice lost its dominance over the trade in the middle east, which created new opportunities for merchants.
  • The increase in the cloth trade affected England in many ways
  • children, men, and women all had different skills involved in taking a sheep and turning into 'English broadcloth'
  • Boys could cut the sheep's wool off the sheep
  • Women could spin the wool into threads
  • Men would weave the threads into cloth
  • Skilled workers could 'finish' the cloth by dying it or softening it with various methods.
  • As the cloth trade grew, one group of merchants and one city came to dominate the entire trade 
  • The Merchant Adventurers took total control over ALL the trade by the end of Henry's reign
  • ALL the trade happened through London
  • At the beginning of the reign, trade with Spain and Italy happened through the port at Southampton, and Trade with Norway/Sweden/Denmark happened through the port at Hull
  • However, it was cheapest through London, and as the Merchant Adventurers did more and more trade, they were able to destroy the competition from merchants in Southampton and Hull

  • Under Somerset, four key changes were made in 1547 and 1549

  • In February 1547, one month after Henry VIII died, Nicholas Ridley (Bishop of Rochester) made a speech declaring that 'images' (paintings/statues/stained glass/mosaics) in churches were bad.

  • A new prayer book was made that all churches had to have.
  • A prayer book contains all the prayers that are said at different times of day, and at different moments of life, e.g marriage/death/baptism/before going to bed
  • The wording of the prayer book set up how people thought about the meaning of the prayers, and therefore the exact way they thought about God and religion.
  • Therefore it was important to control the wording so that people's beliefs could be 'nudged' towards protestantism.
  • Two key differences to the old prayer book
  • The prayers for the 'mass' (bread and wine) were now said in English
  • Any prayer about doing good deeds was worded differently

  • The prayer book made this protestant belief clear. This belief is called 'justification by faith alone' (justification means getting saved/going to heaven)

WAY IT CHANGED TWO - Under Northumberland (in charge 1551-1553) HUGE religious changes were made.

  • The changes made in 1552/1553 were the biggest wave of protestant changes in English history
  • They are based around two key things: the new prayer book of 1552, and the 'Forty Two Articles'
  • Prayer Book of 1552
  • This was much more protestant than the Somerset prayer book of 1549, or any changes under Henry VIII
  • The mass (bread and wine) was changed
  • It was made clear in the prayers that the bread and wine DO NOT become the body and blood of Christ.
  • THis is the end of a Catholic doctrine called 'transubstantiation' (the transformation of the substance)
  • The worshippers at church still had to kneel when they took the bread and wine, but the prayers made it clear that you are NOT worshipping the bread and wine, which is what Catholics were doing before.                
  • the part of the prayer book that made this clear was right at the very beginning in something called the 'Black Rubric'
  • There were only two sacraments again (five removed).
  • Baptism and the Eucharist remained
  • But the sacraments that remained were changed to be more protestant.
  • Baptism for example: the baby was dipped only once in the water, not three times, and no holy oil was used.
  • Protestants want less emphasis on baptism, as only Christ can save you, not special oil/water/sacraments.
  • The Eucharist was now called 'holy communion' (as it still is today)
  • The bread and wine was blessed on a WOODEN TABLE not a STONE ALTAR. this removed the sense that some 'magic sacrifice' was happening'
  • Clothes of priests and music in services were made simpler and less fancy (protestants don't like too much decoration as it distracts from the importance of the Bible, which should be the focus)
  • The Forty Two Articles of 1553 were another big change
  • It was a list of forty two 'official beliefs' ('articles' = bullet points)
  • (rather than prayers that were written to suggest beliefs)
  • Most of the beliefs were the same as the 1552 prayer book, but the Forty Two Articles made the religious changes really clear.
  • A good example is the very protestant belief that if you are not chosen by God before birth (even before the creation of the universe) to be saved then you cannot go to heaven no matter how hard you try.
  • This is called Predestination.
  • The prayer book and the forty articles were reinforced by an 'Act of Uniformity' in 1552 made it illegal to not have the prayer book in every church.
  • SO
  • The prayer book of 1552, the Forty Two Articles 1553, and the Act of Uniformity that enforced them on ALL churches, was the completion of the 'protestant reformatiion' in England.
  • After this, under Mary and then Elizabeth, England went backwards away from protestantism and was never quite as protestant as at this moment (1553)

  • 'Statute' = law made by parliament
  • 'Artificers' = workers and craftsmen'

  • Fix wages at a maximum level, enforced by Justices of the Peace (like policemen)
  • Force workers to work at harvest time, even if wages offered were not high
  • (this meant forcing unemployed workers who were waiting for a better job at the highest possible wage)
  • Force craftsmen to work as apprentices for seven full years before being able to work on their own (and therefore work for less money as apprentices are paid less)

  • The cloth industry had a few bad years in early 1560s

  • The 'deserving poor' were those who couldn't work. E.g. widows/disabled people/small children
  • The 'undeserving poor' were those who (it was claimed) refused to work
  • The Statute of Artificers aimed at helping the poor
  • The poor relief (money collected from villagers to help the poor) was more effectively collected, as new penalties for not paying were imposed (prison)
  • Minimum wages were set in areas where the wages were so low that even those who worked were basically homeless.
  • The Statute also tried to CONTROL the poor
  • Many people were wandering around England looking for work, and the Statue made it illegal to do this.
  • Therefore, if you were looking for work, the new law said that you could only look around in the area where you were born.

WAY IT CHANGED FOUR - The government took in even more 'debased' low value coins, slowing down the inflation

  • It was under Elizabeth's early reign (1558-1562) that the really big actions to withdraw the low value coins from the country were taken
  • It took many years for the effects to be seen, but Elizabeth's ministers took more action to improve the silver content in coins than any of the Kings/Queens before her.

  • Whether Elizabeth would marry

  • All priests were asked in 1559 to take the oath, but in 1563 parliament wanted ANOTHER oath, to make sure that all those that had refused the oath the first time to be arrested and removed from society/killed
  • Elizabeth accepted the will of parliament, but then cleverly didn't ask for the second oath to be taken by the priests of England.
  • This saved the priests who would have refused embarrassment/the need for conflict
  • HOWEVER
  • After 1570 laws were added to make Catholicism even weaker in England
  • LAW ONE
  • 1571, 'Bulls etc. from Rome Act'
  • When the Pope excommunicated Elizabeth (officially kicked out of the Roman Catholic Church) in 1570, a law was passed by parliament to make the Pope's orders ('Papal Bulls') illegal in England
  • Anyone found with a copy of a Papal bull (an order from the Pope), or publishing one, would be executed for treason
  • LAW TWO
  • 1581, 'Act to Retain the Queen's Majesty's Subjects in their Due Obedience''
  • This increased the fine for people not attending church on Sunday to £20 (£4000 today)
  • NOTE: not going to church was a sure sign that someone was secretly worshipping in the catholic style at home
  • Any priest caught saying mass would likely be executed
  • 15 priests were caught and executed between 1581 and 1582

WAY IT CHANGED TWO - The 'puritans' tried to push the Church of England to be even more protestant than agreed in Elizabeth's 'religious settlement' of 1559

  • By 1559, not every aspect of the Church of England was fully protestant
  • The people that wanted to go to FULL protestantism in ALL aspects of the the church were called 'puritans'
  • They were called this because they wanted to 'purify' the church of all the remaining Roman Catholic features
  • Between 1563 and 1603 There was a fight between Elizabeth and the puritans over whether to push the Church of England to become even more protestant
  • Elizabeth won the fight by 1583, and the puritans in the church were ultimately destroyed.
  • SO... what was still 'not protestant enough' about the Church of England in the 1560s?
  • ONE
  • The Church was still governed by the Queen after 1559 (like under Henry VIII). Puritans wanted the priests, not the Queen, to have more control over the Church 
  • Puritans saw the Elizabeth Settlement of 1559 LINK as giving too much control to politicians (i.e. to the Queen and her council) and not enough power to the priests
  • ULTIMATELY what the puritans wanted was the priests to be in charge so they could make the Bible itself the only true guide in life, not politicians
  • Why is it more protestant to want the Queen to have less power over the church?
  • When there is a disagreement between two Christians about what is right/wrong, then they can... OPTION ONE: go to the Bible to decide what to do, OR OPTION TWO: get the head of the church/the bishops to decide for them.
  • The Puritans want OPTION ONE
  • So therefore they don't want Elizabeth as 'Supreme Governor' ordering Christians to believe what/worship how she wants. They want the priests at the lower levels to use the Bible itself to decide things.

  • TWO
  • The clothes that priests wore were still too fancy (and therefore too catholic-y for the puritans to accept
  • Extreme protestants want PLAIN clothes so as to emphasise that this material world is not important
  • Less extreme protestants (like Elizabeth) were happy with the clothes having bright colours and being lined with gold. They thought this would help Christians experience the glory and richness of God.
  • The 'Vestiarian Controversy' of 1566 was the key fight over this (puritans vs Elizabeth)
  • Elizabeth, through her Archbishop of Canterbury Matthew Parker, tried to force all the priests to wear more plain clothes.
  • This was made clear in Matthew Parker's 'Advertisements' of March 1566
  • It said all priests HAD to dress exactly the same, as well as ensure all church services were exactly the same (and less plain than many priests wanted).
  • There would be no freedom for the priests to decide for themselves
  • 37 London priests refused to obey. They were fired.
  • Elizabeth was enforcing the religious settlement of 1559.
  • It was one thing to announce the settlement using parliament in 1559, and another thing to actually get all the priests of England to actually follow every aspect of it (e.g. clothes).
  • SO did the puritans win, or did Elizabeth win?
  • In the end, Elizabeth won,
  • She remained in control of the church as Supreme Governor, in control of how people in England worshipped, and what people believed
  • She successfully ensured that priests didn't wear clothes that were too plain. Priests' clothes are still quite fancy in England to this day.
  • HOWEVER
  • Puritanism didn't go away. It was only destroyed as a force within the church, but there were many puritans in parliament all the way through her reign, and beyond. The puritans ended up killing the King and taking over the country for 10 years in 1650s

WAY IT CHANGED THREE - The 'Presbyterians' (extreme puritans) started trying to get rid of all Bishops entirely

  • The most extreme protestants were called 'Presbyterians'
  • The more extreme the Puritans the more less they wanted the Queen or the Bishops to boss Christians around/tell them what to believe
  • The most extreme Puritans were called 'Presbyterians' who didn't even want bishops (as bosses of the church) to exist at all, and simply let all Christians choose what they wanted to do/believe.
  • Presbyterians claim that because there are no bishops and archbishops mentioned in the Bible itself, then they shouldn't exist at all in England
  • For them, everything is about 'is it in the Bible? If not... get rid of it'
  • 'Presbyter' is a Latin word for 'elder'. The 'elders' would just advise Christians, rather than boss them around.
  • The opposite of presbyterian is 'episcopalian' which means having bishops take all the power (episcopus = 'bishop')
  • So presbyterians DON'T WANT ANY CHURCH HIERARCHY AT ALL
  • No individual Christian (e.g. a bishop) can claim to have authority to tell any other Christian what to believe
  • INSTEAD they have 'synods', which are like mini church parliaments that provide guidance (not strict orders that have to be followed) for other Christians. These parliaments have lots of people in them, so no ONE Christian has any power over another.

  • Elizabeth's Settlement in 1559 was known as the 'via media' (middle way) between having, on the one hand, The Pope totally control what people believe, and on the other, having individual Christians just use the Bible and directly ask God to guide each individual.
  • SO...
  • Although Elizabeth I's Church of England was much more protestant than The Catholic Church, and emphasised the Bible as the most important guide to life, there were still bishops and archbishops (e.g. Matthew Parker, the Archbishop of Canterbury) telling Christians how to worship, and what to believe (e.g. 'do unbaptised babies go to heaven?', or 'can priests get married?', or what EXACTLY is and isn't a sin etc.)

  • The Presbyterians tried to change the way the Church of England worked
  • There were three waves...
  • WAVE ONE: 1570
  • Within Cambridge University there were lots of presbyterians...
  • Thomas Cartwright published lots of pamphlets attacking the head of the university John Whitgift (at that time head of Cambridge University) saying that the Elizabethan Settlement was not acceptable (i.e. it was not protestant enough)
  • Thomas Cartwright wanted a presbyterian church with more freedom for Christians to use ONLY the Bible to guide them
  • WAVE TWO: 1572
  • John Field published the 'First Admonition' and the 'Second Admonition' (pamphlets) calling for a Presbyterian structure to the Church
  • WAVE THREE: 1580s
  • There was a group of ultra extreme presbyterians called 'Separatists'. They wanted to totally break away from the Church of England (not just reform it).
  • Robert Browne led some Separatists in Norwich in the 1580s
  • Henry Barrow led a group of Separatists in London in the 1580s
  • These Presbyterians were ultimately defeated by Elizabeth by the 1580s, and didn't get to change/break away from the Church of England
  • A key defeat was 1583 when the new Archbishop of Canterbury John Whitgift issued the 'Three Articles
  • This was a set of three minimum commitments that all members of the Church of England had to follow
  • ONE
  • Accept Elizabeth as the head of the church
  • TWO
  • Accept every idea in the 1559 prayer book (a guide on how to pray, but really a set of interpretations of Christianity that made clear Elizabeth's views on what type of Christianity was correct)
  • THREE
  • Accept the 'Thirty Nine Articles'  LINK
  • These were 39 key principles of Elizabeth's version of protestantism
  • e.g. the rejection of transubstantiation (the bread and wine don;t become the body and blood of Christ, they're just symbols)
  • e.g. there were only TWO sacraments (marriage and the eucharist), unlike the seven performed by Catholics
  • After these three articles were issued/announced, presbyterianism became less and less powerful
  • It was clear that Elizabeth would put presbyterians in prison if they kept fighting her
  • PLUS the most extreme presbyterians, the separatists, were executed for treason.
  • Another reason presbyterianism declined is three key supporters of presbyterianism within Elizabeth's council died of old age
  • Francis Walsingham, Walter Mildmay, and Robert Dudley (the Earl of Leicester) all died removing key supporters of presbyterianism that could have influenced Elizbaeth

WAY IT CHANGED FOUR -  Art and Culture transformed in many ways during Elizabeth's reign

  • The period between 1580 and 1610 was the greatest period in all history, in any country, for poetry and drama.
  • Shakespeare wrote his works (37 plays and 154 poems called 'sonnets') between 1590 and 1610
  • Shakespeare's plays are recognised as the greatest achievement by any writer, ever. No other country has ever produced anything as profound and timeless. This all happened during Elizabeth's reign (and a few years after her death)
  • The four great tragedies, Hamlet, Macbeth, Othello, and King Lear are seen as the pinnacle of his achievement, and were written between 1601 and 1606, so right at the very end of Elizabeth's reign
  • One thing that was remarkable about Shakespeare's plays was that they were written for the common people, not just the rich, but were still the highest level of art.
  • In other countries 'high art' was not written and performed for the common people, but for the rich.
  • There were many other great playwrights during this time, many of whom are still studied for English Literature A Level        
  • Thomas Marlowe, Ben Jonson, John Webster, and Thomas Dekker are all also regarded as all time great playwrights
  • More great plays were written during Elizabeth's reign than for all of the 17th, 18th, and 19th centuries combined
  • Music also greatly changed during Elizabeth's reign
  • A new type of choir music was invented, called the 'madrigal'
  • This new type of music had four different and varying musical lines all being sung at once
  • This made the music more complex and creative. This type of music involves lots of music theory regarding how to make all the musical lines fit together.
  • This theory was developed a lot during Elizabeth's reign
  • The two greatest musicians during the reign were Thomas Tallis, and William Byrd

WAY IT STAYED THE SAME ONE - The Church of England defeated all attempts to change it, and by 1603 (end of reign) it was basically the same as in 1559 (settlement at beginning of reign)

  • The key features of the Elizabethan 'religious settlement' were still in place at the end of Elizabeth's reign
  • Elizabeth decided what she wanted English Christianity to be like in 1559 (with some compromise with parliament) and didn't make any more changes for the next 44 years.
  • Elizabeth remained the 'Supreme Governor' of the Church and never backed down and gave away power/control
  • Elizabeth kept the sacraments the same, the key doctrines the same (e.g. rejecting transubstantiation LINK)
  • Elizabeth defeated the puritan attempt to get rid of the authority of bishops, and remove fancy music or fancy clothes for priests.
  • So all these elements stayed the same over the whole reign

WAY IT STAYED THE SAME TWO - The Catholic missionaries sent from Europe failed to convert many people back to Catholicism

  • The Roman Catholic Church attempted to convert as many Englishmen back to Catholicism as they could
  • They did this by sending 'missionaries' to England. Missionaries are people sent to convert people to a religion (that's their 'mission')
  • One famous group of Catholic missionaries were the Jesuits.
  • In 1575 there were 11 Jesuit missionaries in England
  • By 1580 there were 100
  • By 1590 there were 279
  • So we see a constant flow of Catholic missionaries coming to England
  • HOWEVER
  • These missionaries failed to convert almost any English people. So this is a way it STAYED THE SAME
  • What they ended up doing was living with rich people who were Catholics, and then becoming their secret priests so they could secretly worship in the Catholic way at home.
  • Catholicism never became popular among the common people again
  • Catholicism became known as a 'country house religion' as these (small number of) rich people had their private catholic priests in their large country homes, and the common people stuck to protestantism

WAY IT STAYED THE SAME THREE - The Church of England still had its own legal system separate from the non-religious legal system.

  • Despite the religious changes towards protestantism, one hated aspect of the Church before the Tudors was still in place at the end of Elizabeth's reign: The church courts
  • People still had to pay a special tax to the church called the 'tithe'. this was often ten percent of the crops that peasants grew each year.
  • This tithe was hated by the people of England
  • This should remind us that the people of England were still suspicious of priests, and didn't like the church being too involved in their affairs
  • This was similar to before the reformation, when priests who committed crimes would get tried in separate courts from normal people ('laity') and got different and often lesser punishments.

WAY IT STAYED THE SAME FOUR - Many aspects of culture remained the same

  • There were THREE aspects that stayed the same:
  • ONE
  • Right through Elizabeth's reign the vast majority of people of England remained illiterate
  • 20 percent of men were literate in 1500, and it was STILL only 25 percent in 1600
  • 10 percent for women in 1500, and still 10 percent in 1600
  • This is why Shakespeare wrote PLAYS not novels. The people of england couldn't read, so plays were (almost) the only way that stories could be consumed by the people
  • TWO
  • Music remained quite 'catholic-y' in nature
  • Extreme protestants are suspicious of too much music in church services
  • They think you should just read the Bible or listen to a sermon. The music is too much of a reminder of the material world, when you should be thinking about how sinful you are.
  • BUT...
  • The Music in England stayed very similar to the music in Catholic countries, thanks to Elizabeth liking it the 'old' way (not the protestant way)
  • So this is a way it STAYED THE SAME
  • Thomas Tallis and William Byrd were both Catholics, and were tolerated as Catholics by Elizabeth
  • William Byrd even wrote Catholic music for his Catholics who paid him ('patrons') to write special catholic music
  • THREE
  • Landscape paintings were still very rare. All artists over the whole of Elizabeth's reign painted portraits of people, not scenes of nature.
  • Some artists painted groups of people rather than just individuals, and sometimes these portraits were tiny miniatures that went inside a locket. However, the focus of English art was human beings, not nature right till the END of the reign and beyond.
  • The influence of Dutch and northern European artists continued to be equally important as the influence of Italian artists. The balance REMAINED THE SAME over the whole period.
